It's been a while since I've had some time to reflect and write about what I have been studying. I recently completed going through RAG, or Retrieval Augmented Generation. We all know that LLMs, or AI in general, have been trained on a vast variety of data, and developers constantly add publicly available information. However, there are 2 major limitations of LLMs that still exist today.

- **Private Data:** This refers to data that is not publicly available, and therefore won't be included in the training data.
- **Current Events:** Although developers keep adding data, it is a tedious, time-consuming, and expensive process to add and maintain up-to-date information. To overcome this difficulty, training data is injected in intervals. So if you ask an AI about something very recent, it may have no knowledge of it and could hallucinate, responding with information that is false.

## The Problem of Hallucination

Let's take an example of hallucination.

Imagine you ask an AI:

> "What are the latest features in iPhone 18?"

If the AI was trained before the iPhone 17 was announced, it has no data on it whatsoever. However, instead of admitting it doesn't know, it might respond with:

> "The iPhone 17 comes with a 108MP camera, a titanium body, and a new A19 chip with 30% faster performance than its predecessor."

This sounds incredibly realistic — it follows the pattern of how Apple typically markets its products. But every single detail here is made up. The AI is drawing on patterns from previous iPhone releases and presenting fabricated specs as fact. **This is a hallucination.**

## What is RAG?

To overcome this, we use RAG, in which we supply the LLM with data that may be up-to-date or private (not publicly available), in order to tailor the LLM to our needs.

To accomplish this, we carry out 4 major steps:

1. **Load** — Extract the data from a document.
2. **Split** — Once the data is extracted, it needs to be split into chunks of manageable size.
3. **Embed** — Convert the data into vectors, which are in a format readable by the computer.
4. **Store** — Store the vectors for fast retrieval.

Let's deep dive into the first step.

---

## Step 1: Load

The loading step is responsible for extracting data from various sources — text files, PDFs, websites, and more. LangChain provides dedicated loaders for each source type.

```python
# Loading document

# From a .txt file
text_loader = TextLoader("long-doc.txt")
text_doc = text_loader.load()

# From a PDF file
pdf_loader = PyPDFLoader("sample-report.pdf")
pdf_doc = pdf_loader.load()

# From a website
web_loader = WebBaseLoader("https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/large-language-models")
web_doc = web_loader.load()

# Combine all documents together
all_docs = text_doc + pdf_doc + web_doc
print(f"Total documents loaded: {len(all_docs)}")
```

**Output:**
```
Total documents loaded: 12
```
